# Case Study: Phishing and Social Engineering to Steal Credentials

**Attack Vector:** Phishing / Social Engineering

**Written by:** Akshat singh | 25BCE10857

---

### 1. Understanding the Idea

Phishing is basically a trick where attackers pretend to be trusted organizations to get sensitive information from people. Instead of using technical hacking methods, they rely more on how people think and react—like creating urgency, fear, or trust—to get what they want. The goal is usually to steal login details, financial data, or personal information. 

A common method used in these attacks is sending an email with a link that looks normal but actually leads somewhere malicious. When the user clicks on it, they are taken to a fake website that looks almost identical to something real, like a university login page.

Because the message often creates panic (like saying access will be lost), the student quickly enters their username and password without thinking much. That’s when the data gets captured. In many cases, the site even redirects the user to the actual website afterward so nothing feels suspicious. Meanwhile, the attacker already has the credentials and can log in instantly.

---

### 2. Who Is Most Vulnerable?

Students and university staff are often easy targets, and there are a few reasons for that:

* **Stress and Deadlines:** Students are usually busy and under pressure, so they don’t always take time to carefully check emails.

* **Trust in Authority:** If an email looks like it’s from “IT Services” or administration, people tend to believe it without questioning much.

* **Mobile Usage:** Many students check emails on their phones, where it’s harder to notice fake links or suspicious sender details.

---

### 3. What Happens After an Attack?

Once an account is compromised, things can quickly get worse:

* **Spreading the Attack:** Hackers can use the student’s email to send phishing messages to others, making it look even more believable.

* **Access to Sensitive Data:** Student portals can contain personal details, schedules, and even financial information.

* **Risk to the Institution:** If attackers go further, they might access larger systems, which could lead to ransomware attacks or data theft.

* **Personal Impact:** The student has to deal with stress, securing accounts, and possible identity theft, along with losing access to important platforms.
